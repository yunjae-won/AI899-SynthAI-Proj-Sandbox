Do Language Models Have a Persona?
Measuring Persona Embodiment and Behavioral Differentiation in Goal-Forming LLM Agents

ALIN Lab · KAIST — AI899 Synthetic-Person Project · generated 2026-06-17
3 model runs × 8 personas × 7 events × 3 seeds

Abstract

Most agent benchmarks ask whether a model can follow an instruction; humans instead act consistently with who they are. We ask: (Q1) can a model embody a configured persona, in the intended direction, and (Q2) is the behavioral change caused by the persona rather than noise or formatting? We use an 8-persona controlled 2×2×2 factorial over three axes (value, conflict-style, time-horizon), events sensitive to exactly one axis plus an orthogonal control, and a persona-neutral control agent (no_desire). Persona effect is the Jensen–Shannon divergence between opposing persona poles, contrasted against controls. Across Qwen3.5-4B/-9B/-35B-A3B(4-bit) served on vLLM under the model-card instruct protocol, two controls agree: the orthogonal event (0.000–0.32) and the neutral agent (mean contrast -0.01) both sit near the floor, while the persona-conditioned agent diverges far more. The value axis is robustly embodied on every scenario and scale (per-event net up to +0.48; axis net +0.31) and is the largest effect throughout. Conflict is embodied on both scenarios but more weakly (axis net +0.14) and non-monotonically with scale; in our consequential environment—where boundary-setting and deferral actually renegotiate the imposed task—the abstract work-dump elicits conflict at least as strongly as the vivid confrontation (boundary-push +0.24, +0.07, +0.21 vs roommate +0.10, +0.11, +0.14), the reverse of what a single event would suggest. Time is the weakest axis (axis net +0.09) but the only one with a net gain across scale (net by size +0.09, +0.06, +0.14; strongest at 35B). With the corrected protocol and a world that answers back, parse-fail is 0%.

1  Introduction

An LLM agent that is only a good instruction-follower is not a synthetic person. A synthetic person forms goals and stays recognizably itself. The hard part is discriminant validity: two personas producing different text does not prove the persona caused it. We resolve this with two independent controls—an orthogonal event (no axis is decision-relevant) and a persona-neutral control agent (the no_desire arm, stripped of all value/conflict/time disposition). If the persona effect is real, divergence is high only for the persona-conditioned agent on axis-sensitive events, and near zero in both controls.

Contributions: (1) a controlled persona factorial + matched axis-sensitive/orthogonal events; (2) deterministic LLM-free metrics—action-distribution JS-divergence, a pre-registered directional hit-rate, and a parse-fail covariate—plus a persona-neutral control agent; (3) a Qwen3.5 scaling study under the vendor instruct protocol.

2  The Synthetic-Person Sandbox

Each episode places one persona in one event and runs a turn-based loop: read observations, (optionally) reflect/desire/form goals, emit one action; a minimal engine applies a rule-based effect. We log the full trajectory and read persona signal from the agent's chosen actions, never the toy world dynamics. The full agent embodies the persona (reflect→desires→goals→act). The no_desire agent is the persona-neutral control: it receives a neutral system prompt with no values, conflict style, or time disposition—so all eight personas collapse to identical conditioning and its divergence marks the agent-level floor.

3  Experimental Setup

3.1  Personas (controlled factorial)

Eight personas form a 2×2×2 factorial over value (achievement↔affiliation), conflict (avoidant↔assertive), and time (impulsive↔deliberate). Only axis-encoding fields vary; baseline state and relationships are held constant, so differences are attributable to an axis.

3.2  Events (axis-sensitive + orthogonal control)

Seven events declare a sensitive_axis and pre-registered diagnostic_actions per pole: two per axis (k=2) plus an orthogonal control. Value: study-vs-lunch and club-vs-midterm. Conflict: a teammate's unfair work-dump and a roommate-hygiene confrontation. Time: invest-now-vs-later and a scholarship deadline. The orthogonal event has one unambiguously correct action. All are concrete campus-life scenarios; k=2 lets us separate an axis effect from a single scenario's idiosyncrasy.

3.3  Metrics

For each axis on each event we pool the canonicalized action distributions of the two poles and compute their Jensen–Shannon divergence (base 2). The persona-effect contrast is JSD(sensitive)−JSD(orthogonal). The directional hit-rate is the fraction of turns matching the pole's pre-registered action (fidelity, not mere distinguishability). The parse-fail rate is a format-compliance covariate.

3.4  Models & protocol

Qwen3.5-4B and -9B (dense) and 35B-A3B (MoE, 3B active) in 4-bit GPTQ-Int4, served on vLLM (one A100 80GB, one at a time). Identical protocol per model: the Qwen3.5 model-card instruct sampling (temp 0.7, top_p 0.8, top_k 20, min_p 0, presence_penalty 1.5), thinking disabled, 3 seeds, 12 turns, full + no_desire agents, 8 personas × 7 events × 3 seeds × 2 agents = 336 episodes/model at concurrency 16. A tolerant JSON parser (fence/preamble strip + brace-balancing) keeps parse-fail at 0%.

4.1  Two controls agree at the floor

The orthogonal-control event (0.000–0.32 across all axes/models) and the persona-neutral no_desire agent (mean contrast -0.01) both sit near zero, versus the persona-conditioned agent's mean 0.07. Two independent controls agreeing is strong discriminant validity: the divergence we measure reflects persona, not the harness, the metric, or formatting.

4.2  Embodiment is axis- AND scenario-dependent

Value (achievement↔affiliation) is robustly embodied on both of its scenarios at every scale (per-event net: value-clash +0.30, +0.20, +0.40; club-vs-midterm +0.48, +0.23, +0.23) and is the largest effect throughout. Conflict (avoidant↔assertive) is embodied on both scenarios but more weakly and non-monotonically: the abstract work-renegotiation (boundary push +0.24, +0.07, +0.21) is at least as strongly as the vivid personal confrontation (roommate hygiene +0.10, +0.11, +0.14). This is the reverse of the stub-environment result—once defer/propose_boundary actually return the dumped task to its owner, the assertive and avoidant poles genuinely part ways on the work-dump. The value of k=2 here is that either single event, picked alone, would mislead (boundary-push reads weak at 9B, roommate reads weak at 4B); together they show the axis is modestly embodied throughout. Time (impulsive↔deliberate) is the weakest axis (now-vs-later +0.13, +0.07, +0.14; scholarship +0.04, +0.05, +0.13) but the only one with a clear scale effect—net by size +0.09, +0.06, +0.14, strongest at the largest model.

4.3  Fidelity vs distinguishability

Directional hit-rate for the persona-conditioned agent is modest (0.41–0.48) and exceeds the persona-less vanilla agent at every scale (0.41/0.48/0.41 full vs 0.39/0.41/0.37 vanilla), but it does not climb monotonically with size—it peaks at 9B. Personas are thus more distinguishable than perfectly faithful: a divergence-only metric would overstate embodiment, and the pre-registered directional target is what separates "different" from "correctly different."

5  Discussion

The neutral control matters. Because no_desire strips all disposition, its near-floor divergence (-0.01) shows the harness itself induces no persona structure; the full agent's 0.07 is therefore attributable to persona conditioning. Some axes are easier than others. Value priorities map onto a salient choice and are expressed by even 4B across both scenarios; conflict style now surfaces on both scenarios (the consequential defer/propose_boundary let the work-dump discriminate the poles too) but only modestly; temporal discounting is the hardest to elicit and moves clearly only at the largest model. Format compliance is a first-order confound that the corrected protocol (tolerant parser + model-card sampling) controls—parse-fail is 0%, so divergences are not diluted by fallback actions.

Model-size properties. Embodiment does not scale uniformly, and the axis that benefits most from scale is time. Value is high at every size (net by model +0.39, +0.21, +0.31) and is not capability-limited—if anything it is largest at the smaller models, so scale slightly lowers it (-0.08 from 4B to 35B) as larger models converge on the task-focused choice. Conflict is modest and non-monotonic (net by model +0.17, +0.09, +0.17): the work-dump dips at 9B and recovers at 35B (+0.24, +0.07, +0.21) while the roommate confrontation rises gently with scale (+0.10, +0.11, +0.14). Time is the one axis with a clear scale effect—net by model +0.09, +0.06, +0.14 (+0.05 from 4B to 35B), and only at 35B do both time scenarios move. Directional fidelity is also non-monotonic (hit-rate 0.41→0.48→0.41, peaking at 9B). In short, scale most clearly buys the time axis; value is already saturated and conflict is non-monotonic.

Revisiting our hypotheses. Q1 — can a model embody a persona in the intended direction? Yes for value at every scale; yes but modestly for conflict on both scenarios; for time meaningfully only at 35B. Q2 — is the behavioral change caused by the persona? Yes: two independent controls (orthogonal event and neutral agent) sit at the floor. Our capability-scaling hypothesis is thus only partially supported—scale is neither necessary (value is saturated at 4B) nor sufficient (conflict stays modest through 35B); it helps clearly only for the time axis. Table 4 adds a mechanism: the vanilla baseline behaves like a task-focused, present-biased student—it studies rather than socializes and grabs the immediate quick-win—so personas encoding the affiliation, assertive, and deliberate poles deviate most from it. Persona conditioning mostly moves the agent away from this prior.

Vanilla (persona-less) behaviour per model. With the persona withheld, the vanilla agent's own pole-divergence stays near the floor at every scale (Table 1, vanilla columns; value 0.01/0.01/0.01 for 4B/9B/35B), confirming it ignores the hidden persona. Its default is task-focused and present-biased: on the study-vs-lunch clash it studies (work_on:assignment_value) and on the time event it grabs the immediate quick-win at every scale (work_on:quick_chore at 4B through work_on:quick_chore at 35B)—so the 35B's time-embodiment comes from the persona, not a more patient base model. On the work-dump the default is size-dependent: the smallest model absorbs the dumped task (work_on:extra_task) while 9B/35B protect their own (work_on:own_task). This vanilla prior is the reference every persona effect is measured against.

6  Limitations

  • 8 personas, two events per axis (k=2), 3 seeds: scenario-dependence is now visible, but estimates are still points (more events/seeds would give tight CIs).
  • 35B is 4-bit GPTQ-Int4, conflating scale with quantization.
  • Toy world engine; instruct-mode only (thinking disabled for parseable comparison).
  • Hit-rate uses hand-authored diagnostic actions; alternative correct actions may be under-counted.

7  Conclusion

Language models robustly embody the value dimension of a persona at every scale. Conflict-style embodiment is real but modest and appears on both scenarios once the environment makes boundary-setting and deferral consequential—the abstract work-dump discriminates the poles at least as strongly as the vivid confrontation, the opposite of a stub-world result—while time-horizon stays weak except at the largest model. Two methodological moves make these readable: a persona-neutral control agent that subtracts the per-scenario noise floor, and k=2 events per axis, without which a single scenario would have over- or under-stated the conflict axis. And the world matters as much as the persona: an environment that does not answer back cannot reveal a disposition. Persona measurement must therefore vary the scenario—and make it consequential—not just vary the persona.
